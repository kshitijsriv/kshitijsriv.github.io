Network Connectivity mapping

One of the problems that I worked on for my master’s thesis was detecting bus breakdown events that lead up to traffic congestions. And while working on this problem, one of the difficulties was the intermittent nature of GPS data. We identified inconsistencies in the GPS data frequency of the same bus. For instance, a bus would send data at a 10-second interval for some period and then go blank for a few minutes and then come back to the usual 10-second interval. This behavior was consistent in buses sharing similar routes. Investigating more led us to identify the issue arising over the same stretches of roads. These buses transmit data over cellular networks. And similar to places where we have low network connectivity, there are regions where the GPS devices cannot send data due to some connectivity constraint.

To identify these regions, I tried to model the situation as a graph across Delhi. Bus stops act as nodes in this graph, and consecutive bus stops on a route have an edge connecting them. I tried to quantity the network quality between two nodes as the number of GPS data points between them. I then tried to classify the region as a good, moderate, or poor connectivity region. This is what the map looked like.

As evident, it is not quite possible to clearly identify the connectivity quality of a region. But it does look cool. Then it clicked me; what we really want to see is the density of GPS data points in a region, and what better way to do it than a heatmap. So, I took GPS data for all buses across Delhi for three days and simply plotted a heatmap on a map. And this is what the connectivity heatmap looked like.
